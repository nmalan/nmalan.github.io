About

I am an oceanographer interested in the edge of stuff — where the deep ocean meets the land, how extreme events translate into impacts, and how physical dynamics drive the movement and vertical motion that shape life in the sea. This has pulled me into work on ocean extremes, coastal variability, cross-shelf exchange, and western boundary current dynamics.

I think a lot about marine heatwaves — how we define and detect them, and what's happening beneath the surface where ecosystems actually live — alongside the regional quirks of the East Australian and Agulhas Current systems, where I keep coming back to how eddies, meanders, and boundary currents move heat, nutrients, and living things between the open ocean and the shelf, and what that means for the ecosystems caught up in the exchange.

I'm based at the Climate Change Research Centre at UNSW Sydney, working with Alex Sen Gupta. I did my PhD at the University of Cape Town and still collaborate actively with researchers there. I run a marine heatwave seminar series, do some teaching, and work with some great students.

Research

Marine Heatwaves, their subsurface structure and other extremes

Marine heatwaves are typically measured at the ocean surface, yet their subsurface expression can differ dramatically in intensity, timing, and spatial extent. My research investigates how extreme temperatures penetrate below the mixed layer, their links to mesoscale eddies and boundary current dynamics, and their ecological implications. More broadly, I study coastal marine extremes including the salinity extremes that follow extreme rainfall events and coastal flooding.

Western Boundary Currents

I have worked extensively on both the East Australian Current and the Agulhas Current system — studying eddy-driven cross-shelf transport, current meanders, long-term temperature trends, and the role of boundary currents in driving coastal climate extremes along the Australian and South African coastlines.

Cross-shelf Transport & Coastal Processes

Understanding how heat, nutrients, and biota are exchanged between the open ocean and the continental shelf is central to predicting coastal ecosystem responses to climate change. I combine multi-platform observations (gliders, moorings, satellite data, boat-based surveys) with numerical models to quantify these exchanges and their drivers.

Ocean Observing Systems

Long-term, high-quality ocean observations underpin much of my research. I have worked closely with IMOS — Australia's Integrated Marine Observing System — including as Deputy Leader of the NSW Moorings programme (2018–2023), contributing to the design, deployment, and data stewardship of Australia's sustained coastal observation network. I am committed to open science and reproducible, observation-based research.

Physical–Biological Coupling

Physical dynamics shape biological productivity across a range of scales — from mesoscale eddies that modulate nutrient supply to boundary current shifts that restructure phytoplankton communities. My work in this area includes Lagrangian particle tracking to study larval dispersal and cross-shelf connectivity, 1D biogeochemical modelling, and analysis of how mesoscale circulation in the East Australian Current controls chlorophyll concentrations and productivity across the continental shelf.
